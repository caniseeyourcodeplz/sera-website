# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary:** Practice owners and clinic managers running healthcare practices in the United States — physical therapy clinics, dental practices, chiropractors, med spas, and similar appointment-driven small-to-mid healthcare businesses (typically 1–10 locations, 5–50 staff). They are operationally stretched, lose meaningful revenue every week to missed and after-hours calls, and are skeptical of generic SaaS pitches. They evaluate vendors on trust, ROI math, and HIPAA-grade security — not novelty.

**Secondary:** Office managers and front-desk leads who must believe the system will reduce their burden, not add to it. They are wary of tools that "automate" their job in name only.

**Context when using the site:** Researching during a quiet moment between patients, often on mobile. They're not reading — they're scanning for proof that this is built for healthcare specifically and that the math works for their practice.

**Job to be done:** Decide quickly whether Sera is credible enough to book a demo. The site must answer "Is this real, is it for me, and what will it cost?" within 60 seconds of landing.

## Product Purpose

Sera is an AI receptionist for healthcare practices. It answers every inbound call (during hours, after hours, overflow), books appointments into the practice's existing scheduler, hands off to humans for anything sensitive, and delivers structured transcripts and summaries to the front desk. The category position: "Answer Every Call. Keep the Revenue."

The website's job is to convert qualified clinic owners into demo bookings. Success looks like: a clinic owner lands, sees a credibility signal in the first scroll, hears a real call sample, understands the ROI, and books a demo without needing a sales call to feel safe. Secondary: pricing transparency reduces qualification overhead.

## Brand Personality

**Three words:** Trustworthy, surgical, calm.

**Voice:** Direct, outcome-led, never hyped. Reads like a senior operator who's seen ten of these tools and is unimpressed by demos but cares deeply about phone coverage. Speaks the clinic owner's language — "missed calls," "front desk," "no-shows" — not platform-speak.

**Emotional goals:** Confidence (this team knows healthcare), relief (your phones will stop being a leak), authority (HIPAA, real metrics, not vapor). Avoid: excitement, novelty, futurism.

**Reference lane:** Stripe's restraint applied to healthcare. Linear's sharpness applied to a B2B service. Editorial-medical seriousness — Cleveland Clinic-grade trust without Cleveland Clinic's institutional staleness.

## Anti-references

The site must explicitly NOT look or sound like:

- **Generic SaaS landing pages** — bento-box grids, minimal cards endlessly repeated, identical icon + heading + paragraph blocks, "Trusted by" rows of greyed-out logos with no story.
- **AI-tool marketing** — purple-to-pink gradients, glowing orbs, "Meet your new AI [role]" hero copy, "powered by GPT" badges, neon-on-black aesthetic.
- **Generic clinic stock photos** — smiling-receptionist-in-scrubs, doctors-in-white-coats-fist-bumping, blurred-hospital-corridor backgrounds. Every healthcare site does this. We don't.
- **Bright, playful colors** — cyan, electric blue, lime, hot pink. Healthcare buying decisions are serious. Saturated kids'-app palettes break trust.
- **Flashing animations or dramatic motion** — auto-rotating carousels, parallax stacks, scroll-jacked sequences, video backgrounds.
- **Vague CTAs** — "Learn More," "Click Here," "Get Started," "Sign Up." Every CTA must say what happens next.
- **Multiple equal-weight CTAs per section** — confuses the user, dilutes intent.
- **Hidden pricing** — "Contact Sales for a Quote." Practice owners read that as expensive, opaque, slow. Red flag for B2B healthcare.
- **Feature-focused copy** — "AI-powered conversational platform with NLU." Replace with outcomes — "Answers calls. Books appointments. Texts back. While you sleep."
- **Medical jargon without explanation** — assume the reader runs the practice but doesn't read AI white papers.

## Design Principles

1. **Outcomes over features.** Every headline, sub-headline, and CTA states a result the practice owner gets, not a capability the platform has. Replace verbs of capability ("supports," "enables," "leverages") with verbs of outcome ("answers," "books," "recovers").

2. **Trust through specificity.** Generic credibility signals are noise. Specific ones are signal: "Recovered $42K in missed-appointment revenue at Summit PT in 90 days" is worth more than "Trusted by 150+ clinics." HIPAA-compliant claims must show how, not just say so.

3. **Scannable, not skimmable.** Practice owners read in 10-second bursts between patients. Headlines must carry the argument alone. Body copy should reward the second pass, not require the first.

4. **One primary action per surface.** Every section earns one CTA. Secondary actions are allowed but must visually defer (outline button, text link). No section presents two equal-weight buttons.

5. **Healthcare gravitas, not healthcare cliché.** The visual register sits closer to a medical journal or premium B2B finance product than a wellness app. We borrow the seriousness without the sterility.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG 2.1 AA minimum** across all body and headline text. Healthcare buyers include practice owners across age and ability ranges.
- **Contrast:** Body text contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1; large display text ≥ 3:1. Verified explicitly for the new charcoal-on-cream and green-on-charcoal combinations.
- **Reduced motion:** All scroll-driven and float animations must respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.
- **Type sizing:** Body text floor of 16px on desktop, 17–18px on mobile. No 12px secondary copy.
- **Tap targets:** Mobile CTAs ≥ 44×44px. Phone-number CTAs are tappable links.
- **Color is not the only signal.** Status (success, warning, error) must carry an icon or label, not only a color.
- **HIPAA compliance is a content concern, not just a legal one.** All testimonial imagery, screenshots, and demo recordings must be free of any PHI before publication.
